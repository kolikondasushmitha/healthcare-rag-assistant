1. Definition

Diabetes mellitus is a chronic metabolic disorder characterized by persistent high blood glucose (hyperglycemia) due to defects in insulin secretion, insulin action, or both. Glucose is the primary source of energy for the body, and its regulation is essential for normal functioning.

2. Role of Insulin and Glucose Metabolism
Insulin is a hormone secreted by the pancreatic beta cells.
It helps glucose move from the bloodstream into cells (muscle, fat, liver).
In diabetes:
Either insulin is absent (Type 1)
Or cells become resistant to insulin (Type 2)
This leads to glucose accumulation in the blood.
3. Types of Diabetes
3.1 Type 1 Diabetes
Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells
Absolute insulin deficiency
Usually occurs in children or young adults
Requires lifelong insulin therapy
3.2 Type 2 Diabetes
Combination of insulin resistance and relative insulin deficiency
Most common form (90–95% cases)
Strongly associated with lifestyle and genetics
Gradual onset, often undiagnosed for years
3.3 Gestational Diabetes
Occurs during pregnancy due to hormonal changes
Increases risk of complications for mother and baby
May lead to Type 2 diabetes later in life
3.4 Prediabetes
Intermediate stage where blood glucose is higher than normal
High risk of progressing to Type 2 diabetes
4. Pathophysiology (What Happens in the Body)
Type 1:
Immune system attacks beta cells
No insulin production → glucose cannot enter cells
Leads to energy deficiency and fat breakdown (ketone production)
Type 2:
Cells become resistant to insulin
Pancreas compensates by producing more insulin
Over time, beta cells become dysfunctional → reduced insulin
5. Symptoms (Clinical Presentation)
Early Symptoms:
Polyuria (frequent urination)
Polydipsia (excessive thirst)
Polyphagia (increased hunger)
Other Symptoms:
Fatigue
Blurred vision
Slow wound healing
Frequent infections
Severe (especially Type 1):
Weight loss
Ketoacidosis (life-threatening condition)
6. Diagnosis
Tests and Criteria:
Fasting Plasma Glucose (FPG) ≥ 126 mg/dL
Random Plasma Glucose ≥ 200 mg/dL with symptoms
HbA1c ≥ 6.5%
Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT) ≥ 200 mg/dL after 2 hours
7. Acute Complications
7.1 Hypoglycemia (Low Blood Sugar)
Caused by excess insulin or missed meals
Symptoms:
Sweating
Shaking
Confusion
Loss of consciousness
7.2 Hyperglycemia (High Blood Sugar)
Caused by lack of insulin or poor control
Leads to:
Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) (Type 1)
Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State (HHS) (Type 2)
8. Chronic Complications
8.1 Microvascular
Retinopathy → vision loss
Nephropathy → kidney failure
Neuropathy → nerve damage
8.2 Macrovascular
Cardiovascular disease (heart attack)
Stroke
Peripheral artery disease
9. Management
9.1 Lifestyle Modifications
Balanced diet (low glycemic index foods)
Regular physical activity (150 min/week)
Weight management
9.2 Medications
Oral drugs (e.g., metformin)
Insulin injections
Newer therapies (GLP-1 agonists, SGLT2 inhibitors)
9.3 Monitoring
Self-monitoring of blood glucose (SMBG)
Continuous glucose monitoring (CGM)
Regular HbA1c testing
10. Diet and Nutrition
Recommended:
Whole grains
Leafy vegetables
Lean protein
Healthy fats
High fiber foods
Avoid:
Sugary beverages
Refined carbohydrates
Processed foods
Excess salt and saturated fats
11. Prevention (Mainly Type 2)
Maintain healthy BMI
Regular exercise
Balanced nutrition
Avoid smoking and alcohol abuse
Early screening in high-risk individuals
12. Special Considerations
Foot care is critical (to prevent ulcers/amputation)
Regular eye and kidney check-ups
Mental health support (chronic disease burden)
13. Global Impact
Diabetes is a major public health issue
Increasing prevalence due to sedentary lifestyle and obesity
Significant cause of morbidity and mortality worldwide
